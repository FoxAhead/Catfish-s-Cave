War of the Ring

Here are some screenshots from a scenario that I began back in early 2000: War of the Ring. This project came about after working with Harlan Thompson on a revamped and bug-fixed version of his Lord of the Rings scenario for Civ2: Fantastic Worlds. After evaluating the merits of ToT over older versions of the game, Harlan, who had 'retired' from Civ2 at this stage, lost interest in the FW version and persuaded me to make a new Lord of the Rings scenario for ToT. I worked on it for a few months, with Harlan acting in an advisory role. After a break of several months, I briefly revived it in late 2000 before shelving it indefinitely. It may eventually see the light of day. It's a 4-map single-player scenario. Harlan, Techumseh and Boco have, at various times, made significant contributions to the project.

I started the scenario again from scratch in early 2004, this time replacing my old 18 250-tile gigamap with a much larger one (which I've had sitting on my hard disk for a few years). Below is a zoomed out in-game shot (reduced 50%) of my 32 725-tile gigamap of Middle-earth. I'm much more satisfied with this map, due both to its size and the level of horizontal compression. Horizontal compression? The screenshot may look wider than Tolkien's original Middle-earth map, but in fact the ratio of horizontal to vertical tile distances is less than 1. The apparent widening is due to the 2:1 width to height ratio of the game tiles. Maps which maintain a 1:1 tile ratio usually appear badly stretched horizontally in the game map due to this fact (obviously the mini-map is fine) and Middle-earth was no exception. This map is basically a trade-off between aesthetics and maintaining accurate horizontal tile distances.

The screenshots following are from what could be termed an alpha version of the scenario. The unit placement in the first screenshot is staged. The city sizes are unconfirmed. City graphics are by Favoured Flight.

Oh crap! The consequences of the Ring-bearer's failure to leave home. Sorting the Nazgûl AI has proven to be a real pain. Role 0 domain 3 is the favoured option; it displays the same AI as role 0 ground units and cannot occupy enemy cities. Role 0 air units have a nasty habit of selecting targets on the other side of the map, especially if they are also air units. The main drawback is that domain 3 units are unaffected by SAMs or the x4 vs air units flag. The screenshot also illustrates the importance of city walls. In this example, Hobbiton's population dropped by 3 in a single turn - a hefty blow in a scenario with no settler units.

The High Pass: two transport sites provide access to a secondary map, the contents of which shall remain a mystery (Note: mountains are impassable terrain). Although the High Pass was kept open by Grimbeorn and his Beornings, it was watched by agents of the Enemy.
